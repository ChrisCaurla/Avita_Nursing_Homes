Profiting from
Florida's Worst
Nursing Homes

Scroll

In the middle of Florida's theme parks sits Kissimmee, a manicured, sun-drenched city where resort hotels advertise poolside cocktails and the air smells like sunscreen.

Just a 15-minute drive away a different smell welcomes visitors.

"The moment you enter the lobby, you can smell an odor coming from the rooms," says a nursing assistant at Aviata's Kissimmee Gardens nursing home. There aren't enough trash bags to go around, so diapers sit in the same bag for hours.

She is one of eight current and former employees interviewed for this story who work at one of the 52 skilled nursing facilities operated by Aviata Health Group across Florida. They all report bare-bones cost-cutting measures that lead to increases in falls, bedsores, and unhygienic conditions. 

"Sometimes we have to be running from room to room to find diapers," says another worker who has been in the same facility for three years. Workers also complain of a lack of soap, gloves, and towels. Some say they have resorted to bringing toilet paper from home for their patients. Photo reviews scraped from Yelp show feces and urine on the ground, rusted furniture, and bruises that families say are from falls and bedsores. "They call it the doghouse," says another nursing assistant.

An analysis of 13,574 federal inspection records filed against Florida nursing homes over the past three years corroborates the workers' accounts.

There are 694 registered nursing homes in Florida — one of the largest concentrations in the country.

53 of them are operated by the Aviata Health Group, scattered across the state from the Panhandle to Miami.

An analysis of 13,574 federal inspection records filed over the past three years shows Aviata facilities received 43% more citations than the state average.

The majority of the violations mirrored what workers described, such as unhygienic conditions and old equipment. The most common citation is for lack of "a safe, clean, comfortable and homelike environment," a category where Aviata was cited 54% more often than the rest of the nursing homes in Florida. Violations of residents' rights were 51% more common.

The consequences are severe. Federal inspectors issued "Immediate Jeopardy" citations — the most serious CMS level reserved for risks of serious harm or death — 56% more often than the Florida norm.

Despite their record of patient harm, these facilities receive millions in taxpayer money through Medicare and Medicaid programs. And Welltower can hold its properties tax-free, since it is structured as a real-estate investment trust or REIT.

Bill Dean, a lawyer who has sued Aviata facilities dozens of times over the last 25 years, says he has seen the same pattern for over a decade. "They accumulate lawsuits, then they go bankrupt, and come back with a different name," he says. But what's different this time is that early last year, Aviata facilities were acquired by a public company.

Unlike most private nursing home operators, Aviata's finances became public with its 2025 acquisition by Welltower, the nation's largest provider of senior housing. These financial statements give a rare window into the fortunes that come from neglecting patients and workers. Welltower paid Aviata's owners $1.8 billion in 2025 for 85 nursing homes in Florida and similarly substandard ones in Texas, granting its current owners a long-term operating contract.

Welltower's CEO profited from the deal, among the $33 billion in acquisitions that triggered an astounding $821 million in compensation last year. CEO Shankh Mitra brags about the warp speed of his acquisitions, since senior housing is selling at a discount to when its developers overestimated demand, and is expected to soon be insufficient for the rapidly aging U.S. population.

Mitra has quadrupled Welltower's stock price since becoming CEO in 2020. Based in Toledo, Ohio, the $141 billion company is a real-estate investment trust, or REIT, which holds properties tax-free and receives part of its income. Welltower is part of the S&P 500 index, so its shares are widely held by major pension funds and millions of retirees through their 401(k) plans. Like most roll-ups, Welltower saves money by combining back-office functions like accounting and human resources.

Aviata, as a substandard chain dependent on government funding through Medicare and Medicaid, marked Welltower's quiet move to the bottom of the senior market. It seems that substandard nursing homes can be more profitable than its "ultra-luxury" or "trophy" assisted living facilities. Welltower noted that it can earn even more by further cutting expenses, measured per occupied bed. "You've got quality assets: check. Quality operator: check. Cash flow with room to the upside: check," said Co-President and Chief Investment Officer Nikhil Chaudhri in an April 2025 earnings call. Reliant on government revenue from Medicare, its 101 facilities last year produced $172 million in net operating income, or 5.2% of the total from Welltower's more than 2,500 senior communities. That income had swelled to $212 million in the first three months of 2026 alone.

Putting the spotlight on Welltower could pressure it to live up to its purported focus on improving conditions, not just profits. In his April letter to investors, CEO Mitra wrote: "And, to get the most out of our assets, we are placing operations and technology first, with a singular – and maniacal – focus on delighting customers and prioritizing site-level employee satisfaction."
